Burnout is rarely caused by working too hard on any single day. It's caused by a workday shape that never lets you fully stop — the meeting that bleeds into lunch, the notification that follows you to dinner, the "quick check" that becomes an hour. The fix isn't more discipline. It's better structure.

## Willpower is a bad plan

Study after study finds the same thing: people who rely on willpower to manage their attention lose. The ones who thrive change their environment so the good choice is the easy one. Close the tab. Silence the channel. Put the phone in another room. Structure beats intention.

> "You don't rise to the level of your goals. You fall to the level of your systems." — a cliché precisely because it keeps being true.

## Three changes that actually stick

1. **Bookend your day.** A fixed start ritual and a hard stop signal to your brain when work is and isn't happening.
2. **Batch shallow work.** Group email and Slack into two or three windows instead of a continuous drip.
3. **Protect one deep block.** Ninety minutes, no meetings, notifications off. Defend it like a meeting with your most important client.

## The point

A sustainable workday isn't a productivity hack — it's the thing that lets you keep showing up for years instead of months. Change the structure, reduce the friction, and let the willpower go where it's actually needed: the work itself.
