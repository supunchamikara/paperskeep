Most teams treat pricing as a finance exercise: pick a number, defend it in a spreadsheet, revisit it never. But your price page is a product surface — arguably the highest-leverage one you have — and the teams that treat it that way compound advantages the rest never see.

## Price communicates before it charges

A price tells a story about who your product is for. Too low and you signal a toy; too high without proof and you signal arrogance. The number is a message, and most companies send it by accident.

> "Nobody complains about your price. They complain that they don't understand the value. Those are different problems with different fixes."

## Package for the decision, not the feature list

The classic mistake is pricing by feature count. Customers don't buy features; they buy outcomes. Package around the job they're hiring you to do:

- A **starter** tier that gets someone to their first win fast.
- A **team** tier priced to the value of collaboration, not seat math.
- An **enterprise** tier that sells control, security, and a phone number.

## Test it like a feature

You'd never ship a new UI without watching how people use it. Pricing deserves the same rigor — cohort your conversion by plan, watch where people hesitate, interview the ones who almost bought. The price page isn't a spreadsheet output. It's a product you ship, measure, and improve like any other.
